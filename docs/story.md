# Critical 7 - Story & Game Design

## Genre & Tone

**"Office Punk"** - A surreal blend of corporate mundanity and fantasy adventure. Think:
- *The Stanley Parable* - Absurdist office narrative, breaking the fourth wall
- *The Backrooms* - Liminal spaces, the uncanny familiar turned strange
- *Office Space* meets *Final Fantasy*

**Visual Style:** Mixed office and fantasy tilesets. Cubicles next to dungeons. Water coolers and treasure chests. Fluorescent lights and magical torches.

---

## The Company: Nexus Dynamics

### The Elevator Pitch
Nexus Dynamics is a mid-sized logistics and fulfillment company. They move things - packages, freight, dreams deferred. Founded in 1987, they survived the dot-com bust, the 2008 crash, and a brief pivot to "blockchain-enabled supply chain" in 2017 that no one talks about anymore.

Now they're betting everything on AI.

### The Building
The Nexus Dynamics headquarters is a 7-story office building built in 1994. It has:
- That specific carpet that only exists in offices (gray-blue, slightly damp energy)
- Fluorescent lights that hum at a frequency that makes you forget what year it is
- A basement server room that's been locked since 2003
- An elevator that sometimes stops at floors that don't exist on the buttons
- Conference rooms named after rivers (The Amazon, The Nile, The Styx)

The building is... wrong. Not haunted exactly. More like it's been soaking in decades of fluorescent light, stale coffee, and the quiet desperation of quarterly reviews. The walls remember every failed initiative. The carpet has absorbed a thousand "let's circle back on that."

Sometimes, if you're in the office late enough, you'll notice the hallways are longer than they should be. The break room is in a different place than yesterday. There's a door you've never seen before.

Nobody mentions it. That's not how things work at Nexus Dynamics.

## The Founder & CEO: Harold "Harry" Vance

### Background
Harry Vance started Nexus Dynamics in his garage in 1987 with a single delivery truck and a Rolodex. He's 67 now, still comes in at 6 AM, still wears the same style of blue oxford shirt he's worn since Reagan was president.

He built this company from nothing. Survived three recessions. Outlasted every competitor who said logistics was a "solved problem." He did it through grit, instinct, and an almost supernatural ability to know when a deal was about to go bad.

### The Problem
Harry doesn't understand AI. He's not stupid - he's sharp as a tack when it comes to operations, people, deals. But this... this feels different. Every consultant speaks a language he doesn't recognize. Every vendor promises miracles. His board is pressuring him. His competitors are moving.

He's scared. Not that he'd ever say it.

Three AI initiatives have failed at Nexus Dynamics in the past two years:
1. **Project Falcon** - A demand forecasting tool. Killed after 8 months. No one could explain why the predictions were wrong.
2. **"The Chatbot"** - Customer service automation. Launched, then quietly removed after it told a customer their package was "lost in the space between spaces."
3. **SmartRoute 2.0** - Route optimization. Still technically "in pilot" after 14 months. Everyone knows it's dead.

Harry has authorized one more try. This is it. If this fails, he's done with AI. Maybe done with Nexus entirely.

### Harry's Arc
Harry is not the villain. He's a man out of time, trying to do right by a company he built and the people who depend on it. He can be:
- **An obstacle** - if you don't earn his trust, if you speak in jargon, if you promise things you can't deliver
- **An ally** - if you're honest, if you show him the "why," if you respect what he built
- **A champion** - in the best ending, Harry becomes the executive sponsor who shields you from politics and clears obstacles

His trust is hard to earn and easy to lose.

### Memorable Quotes
- "I've been in this business 37 years. I've never needed a machine to tell me when something smells wrong."
- "My grandfather delivered milk with a horse. I delivered packages with a truck. Now you're telling me a computer's going to do it better?"
- "Everyone who's tried to 'transform' this company has failed. What makes you different?"
- "I don't need a pilot. I need something that works."

## The Project: "Project Atlas"

### The Official Brief
"Leverage AI to optimize end-to-end logistics operations, reduce delivery times by 15%, and cut operational costs by $4.2M annually."

### The Real Problem
Nexus Dynamics is bleeding. Congo is eating their lunch - the logistics giant that started as an online bookstore and now owns half the supply chain. Their routing is inefficient, their demand forecasting is basically "Dave from Operations looks at a spreadsheet," and their customer churn is up 23% year over year.

They need to either transform or die. There's probably 18 months of runway before the board forces a sale or Harry retires in defeat.

### What You're Actually Supposed to Do
No one's quite sure. The scope is "AI transformation." The budget is $750K. The timeline is 16 weeks. The expectations range from "automate everything" (the board) to "don't break anything" (Operations) to "just make Harry happy" (everyone else).

Your job is to figure out what's actually possible, build something that works, and somehow get an organization that's failed three times before to succeed this time.

Good luck.

---

## The Player: You

### Who You Are
You're a consultant brought in to lead Project Atlas. You have experience, but not at Nexus. You know AI, but you don't know this company. You have credibility, but not relationships.

You're an outsider in a building that doesn't like outsiders.

### Why You?
The previous AI leads were all internal. They all failed. Harry's board insisted on bringing in someone from outside - someone without the baggage, without the political entanglements, without the institutional learned helplessness.

You're expensive. You're temporary. And if you fail, you're the perfect scapegoat.

### Your Advantage
You can see what the lifers can't. You can ask the stupid questions. You can push back on "that's how we've always done it." You're not trapped by the building's gravity.

Or at least, you're not trapped yet.

## The Office (As Character)

### The Mundane
- **The Cube Farm** - Rows of identical desks. The hum of typing. The quiet despair of people who've been here too long.
- **The Break Room** - Bad coffee. A refrigerator with things that have been there since the Obama administration. Passive-aggressive notes.
- **The Conference Rooms** - Whiteboards covered in strategies from initiatives past. Projectors that never work on the first try.
- **Harry's Office** - Corner office, 7th floor. Pictures of his family. A model of his first delivery truck. The only plant in the building that's still alive.

### The Strange
As you progress (or as things go wrong), the office starts to shift:
- Hallways that loop back on themselves
- A supply closet that opens onto a forest
- Conference Room C, which no one can find but everyone remembers booking
- The server room in the basement - locked since 2003, but you can hear something humming down there
- Post-it notes in your own handwriting that you don't remember writing
- Coworkers who you're pretty sure weren't here yesterday

The office-punk aesthetic: The surreal isn't fantastical and whimsical. It's the uncanny mundane. A TPS report that's somehow also a treasure map. A spreadsheet that shows data from next quarter. An org chart where your name has always been there, even though you just started.

## Tone Guide

### What This Game Is
- Darkly funny (corporate absurdism)
- Grounded stakes (this project matters)
- Surreal edges (something is wrong with this office)
- Earnest core (you're trying to do something real)

### What This Game Isn't
- Cynical doom (there's hope if you make good choices)
- Tech bro optimism (AI isn't magic, failure is real)
- Pure comedy (the characters have depth)
- Pure horror (the strangeness is unsettling, not terrifying)

### The Central Question
Can you break the cycle? Can you be the project that doesn't fail? Can you navigate the politics, the technical debt, the human resistance, and whatever is happening with this building - and actually deliver something that matters?

90% of AI projects fail.

Will yours?

## Core Game Loop

1. **Walk around** the office environment
2. **Talk to NPCs** and interact with objects/sprites
3. **Make choices** in dialogue that affect the Critical 7 scores
4. **Manage resources** - budget and timeline
5. **See the outcome** - story ending based on final scores

The game tells the story of how your AI initiative turned out based on the cumulative decisions you made.

---

## Project Resources

### Budget
- Start with a fixed budget (e.g., $500K, $1M?)
- Decisions cost money or save money
- Running out of budget = bad ending / early termination
- Some choices are "cheap but risky" vs "expensive but safe"

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│ BUDGET                              │
│ ████████████░░░░░░  $340K / $500K   │
│                                     │
│ Recent:                             │
│  -$50K  Hired external consultant   │
│  -$30K  Data cleanup initiative     │
│  +$20K  Canceled unused licenses    │
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Timeline
- Project has a deadline (e.g., 12 weeks, 6 months?)
- Represented as "days" or "weeks" that pass
- Some decisions take time, some are instant
- Certain events trigger at timeline milestones
- Running out of time = bad ending / incomplete delivery

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│ TIMELINE                            │
│ Week 8 of 12                        │
│ ████████████████░░░░░░░░  67%       │
│                                     │
│ Upcoming:                           │
│  Week 9:  Stakeholder review        │
│  Week 12: Go-live deadline          │
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Critical 7 Scores

Each aspect tracks cumulative decisions:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ STRATEGY                          +15   │
├─────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ ✓ Discussed ROI with Sage         +10   │
│ ✓ Aligned with business goals      +5   │
│ ✗ Promised impossible timeline     -5   │
│ ○ [Locked] Executive buy-in         ?   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

| Score Range | Outcome |
|-------------|---------|
| 30+ | Excellent - major success in this area |
| 15-29 | Good - solid foundation |
| 0-14 | Weak - problems emerge |
| Negative | Critical failure - this aspect tanks the project |

## Decision Design

### Types of Decisions

**Direct choices** - Dialogue options with clear tradeoffs
```
CEO: "We need this live in 3 weeks."

[1] "We can do a limited pilot by then."
    → Strategy +5, Trust +5, Timeline -1 week

[2] "That's not realistic. Here's why..."
    → Trust +10, Strategy -5

[3] "Absolutely, we'll make it happen!"
    → Trust -10, Timeline pressure increases
```

**Resource tradeoffs** - Spend budget/time for score boosts
```
Delta: "We could clean up the customer data, but it'll take 2 weeks and $30K."

[1] "Do it right."
    → Data +15, Budget -$30K, Timeline -2 weeks

[2] "We'll work around the bad data."
    → Data -5, Innovation +5 (creative workarounds)

[3] "Can we do a partial cleanup?"
    → Data +5, Budget -$10K, Timeline -1 week
```

**Hidden consequences** - Some effects aren't obvious
```
You told the CEO "yes" to an impossible timeline.
Three weeks later...
→ Trust -15 (broken promise)
→ Talent -10 (team burnout)
```

### Multi-Aspect Decisions

Most decisions affect multiple scores:
- Rushing = Time saved, but Trust/Talent damaged
- Overspending = Better outcomes, but Budget depleted
- Playing politics = Strategy boost, but Trust cost

## Ending System

The ending is determined by:
1. **Did you finish?** (Budget > 0, Timeline not exceeded)
2. **Overall score** (sum of all 7 aspects)
3. **Critical failures** (any aspect deeply negative?)
4. **Special flags** (CEO support? Team intact? etc.)

### Ending Tiers

**Catastrophic Failure** (Budget depleted OR time ran out OR multiple critical failures)
- Project canceled
- You're reassigned/fired
- Company abandons AI initiatives

**Partial Failure** (Finished but overall score low or critical failures)
- Project "completed" but doesn't scale
- Joins the 90% that never make it to production
- Cautionary tale ending

**Mixed Results** (Finished with moderate scores)
- Some wins, some losses
- Project continues but with challenges
- "Realistic" ending - this is how most projects go

**Success** (Finished with strong scores across the board)
- Project scales to production
- Real business impact
- Team grows, initiative expands

**Exceptional** (High scores + special achievements)
- Transformative success
- Industry recognition
- The rare "everything clicked" outcome

## UI Requirements

- Quick status of Critical 7 (maybe just 7 small bars or icons)

### Detailed Status Screen (pause menu or dedicated UI)
- Each of the 7 aspects expanded
- List of all decisions made and their effects
- Upcoming timeline events

### Decision Log
- Chronological list of all choices
- Shows what each decision affected
- Player can review their journey

## Open Design Questions

1. **Scale of budget/timeline?**
   - $500K over 12 weeks? $2M over 6 months?
   - How granular? Daily? Weekly?

2. **How does time pass?**
   - Automatically after conversations?
   - Player chooses to "advance day/week"?
   - Certain actions consume time?

3. **Can you "grind" for better scores?**
   - Or is content finite (each NPC has limited dialogue)?
   - Replayability through different choices, not repetition?

4. **Is there a "point of no return"?**
   - Or can you always recover with good decisions?

5. **Random events?**
   - "The CEO calls an emergency meeting"
   - "A key team member quits"
   - Adds unpredictability?
